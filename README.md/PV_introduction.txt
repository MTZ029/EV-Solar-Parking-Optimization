## Interpretation of Load vs PV Production

The yearly comparison between building load and PV generation shows
that PV production exceeds building demand during many spring and
summer hours.

These periods create excess PV energy that is currently exported
to the grid.

The annual PV excess is approximately:

113,527 kWh/year

This energy represents the main opportunity for:

- Smart EV charging
- Battery storage
- Energy cost reduction

The next project stages will attempt to utilize this excess energy
instead of exporting it.

PV Sizing Study Interpretation

Increasing the PV size increases annual PV production and load coverage, but also increases excess PV energy.

For small PV sizes, almost all PV production is consumed locally. For example, at 100 kWp, self-consumption is 
98.8%.

For larger PV systems, excess PV becomes significant. At 300 kWp, excess PV is about 113 MWh/year, while at 600
kWp it increases to about 406 MWh/year.

This shows that PV oversizing without battery storage or flexible EV charging leads to large energy exports.
Therefore, EV smart charging and BESS operation are needed to improve PV utilization.

EV-PV Synergy Observation

The ASAP charging profile already shows a natural overlap with PV production.

Most EVs arrive during the morning hours and begin charging immediately.

Because PV production also starts in the morning, a significant amount of charging demand can already be supplied
directly by solar energy.

However, PV production remains very high between approximately 10:00 and 15:00 while EV charging demand decreases.

This indicates that additional PV energy remains unused.

Therefore, shifting part of the charging demand toward midday could increase PV self-consumption and reduce 
excess PV export.

This is the main motivation for smart EV charging optimization.

Corrected EV-PV interaction

The first calculation incorrectly assumed that PV could be used directly by EVs without considering the building 
load.

The corrected approach gives priority to the building load:

PV surplus after building = max(PV production - building load, 0)

Only this surplus can be used by EVs.

For Day 95:

- EV charging demand: 509 kWh
- PV surplus after building demand: 715.31 kWh
- PV directly used by ASAP EV charging: 409.53 kWh
- Grid energy used by EV charging: 99.47 kWh
- Fraction of EV charging supplied by PV: 80.46%

This shows that ASAP charging already has strong PV overlap, but approximately 99.5 kWh still comes from the 
grid. Smart charging can attempt to shift this remaining grid-supplied EV energy toward PV-surplus hours.


Scenario comparison:
-------------------
-------------------

Three representative days were evaluated.

The low-demand day has enough PV surplus to cover all EV charging demand.

The medium-demand day has no PV surplus after building load, so ASAP EV charging is fully supplied from the grid.

The high-demand day has strong PV availability and approximately 80.5% of EV charging is supplied by PV, while
around 99.5 kWh still comes from the grid.

This confirms that optimization should be tested across multiple daily scenarios, because the value of smart
charging depends on both EV demand and PV availability.
