# Notebook 14 – Optimal Sized PV+BESS System with EV Charging

## Objective

The objective of this notebook is to evaluate EV charging strategies using the optimally sized PV+BESS 
system obtained in Notebook 13.

The following charging strategies are compared:

1. ASAP (As-Soon-As-Possible) charging
2. Flexible EV charging

The analysis is performed for representative low-, medium-, and high-demand days.

---

## Input System

The optimally sized PV+BESS system obtained from the OSOPSS joint sizing and scheduling optimization is:

* PV size: 588.77 kWp
* Battery energy capacity: 403.43 kWh
* Battery converter size: 103.25 kW

Battery parameters:

* Charging efficiency: 90%
* Discharging efficiency: 90%
* Maximum depth of discharge: 85%

---

## Representative Scenarios

Three representative days were selected:

| Scenario      | Day |
| ------------- | --- |
| Low demand    | 106 |
| Medium demand | 20  |
| High demand   | 95  |

These days represent different combinations of EV charging demand and PV availability.

---

## ASAP Charging

In the ASAP charging strategy, each EV begins charging immediately after connection and continues until its
required energy has been delivered.

The charging profile is therefore determined solely by vehicle arrival and departure times.

No optimization of charging times is performed.

---

## Flexible Charging

In the flexible charging strategy, EV charging power becomes a decision variable of the optimization problem.

The optimizer determines when charging should occur within each vehicle's parking window while satisfying:

* Required charging energy
* Vehicle availability constraints
* Battery constraints
* Power balance constraints

The objective is to reduce operating costs and improve the utilization of locally generated PV energy.

## Discussion

For the low-demand scenario, the optimally sized PV system produces significantly more energy than required 
by the building and EVs. Consequently, both charging strategies achieve zero grid import and identical 
operating costs.

For the medium-demand scenario, flexible charging provides a small reduction in operating cost. 
The optimizer shifts charging toward more favorable periods, reducing the total daily cost by approximately
1.6 EUR.

For the high-demand scenario, the large PV system is able to cover both building and EV demand. Therefore, 
both strategies achieve zero grid import. However, flexible charging improves local PV utilization and 
reduces exported energy from approximately 1400 kWh to 1372 kWh.

---

## Conclusions

The optimally sized PV+BESS system obtained from the OSOPSS sizing procedure significantly reduces 
dependence on grid electricity.

Flexible EV charging provides the greatest benefit when PV generation is limited and grid energy must be 
imported. When PV production is abundant, both charging strategies achieve similar operating costs, 
although flexible charging can still improve self-consumption by shifting charging toward periods of high 
solar production.

The results indicate that the value of smart charging depends strongly on the relationship between EV 
demand, PV generation, and electricity prices.
